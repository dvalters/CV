From 2013 to 2017 I was a NERC-sponsored PhD student at the University of Manchester, UK. My research developed land surface flooding and erosion modelling software so that it could be integrated with more realistic rainfall inputs such as rainfall radar data, and numerical weather prediction model output. My project involved porting and optimising a numerical model to an HPC (High-performance computing) system. With these improved models, we can better investigate how natural landscapes respond to the severe storms.

Research Interests

I am interested in the way that hydro-geomorphic processes are driven by weather and climate, from the impacts of a single severe storm, to the longer term effcts of climate on landscape evolution. My PhD project: Linking landscape evolution models with high-resolution meteorological data was a collaboration between two departments: the Centre for Atmospheric Science, and Earth Surface Processes at the Univeristy of Manchester.

Much of my work is made possible by the advances made in model resolution, which includes weather forecasting, climate, and digital terrain models. Through using a combination of these different models and data sources, I'm able to extract quantitative information about landscape response to atmospheric processes.

I was co-supervised by Dr Simon Brocklehurst and Professor David Schultz.
